Douglas Engelbart (1925- 2013)

Douglas Engelbart is known as one of the pioneers and innovators of computer technology, particularly for his significant contributions to interactive computer systems. Perhaps his most famous work was the "The Mother of All Demos," presented in 1968, which showcased a series of technological innovations and conceptual advancements. Engelbart was born in 1925, and he began working in the field of computers in the early 1950s. He conducted his most significant work as a researcher at the Stanford Research Institute (now SRI International).One of his most outstanding and significant legacies is the "NLS" or "oN-Line System," which was a precursor to modern computer user interfaces.

NLS was a system that allowed users to interactively work on their computers, using, for example, a mouse-controlled cursor and a graphical user interface. Engelbart's system first introduced the use of the mouse on the computer and generally emphasized interactivity and collaboration. The "1968 demo" transmitted Engelbart's ideas and innovations to a wider audience. In this demo, the NLS system and its capabilities were showcased, including editing text files, using a mouse, real-time communication across multiple locations, and precursor concepts to hyperlinks. These ideas and technologies were instrumental in shaping modern computer systems and the development of the internet.

Engelbart's work and legacy in computer science continue to thrive, serving as inspiration in various fields for those working on computer usage and human-computer interaction development. Following the ideas and technologies presented in "The Mother of All Demos," computer science has taken a direction where efficient communication and collaboration between human users and computers are of fundamental importance.
